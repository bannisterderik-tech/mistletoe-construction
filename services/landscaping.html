Landscaping · Douglas County, OR

Landscaping that works as hard as it looks.

A yard in the Umpqua Valley has two jobs: look good in July and move water in January. We design and install landscaping tailored to your property — grading and drainage that protect your foundation, plantings that survive our wet-dry climate, and fire-wise choices where it matters.

The Roofer's View of a Yard

Water management is the whole game.

  • Grading that sheds water — the ground should slope away from the house, so winter rain drains off instead of pooling at the foundation
  • Downspouts that finish the job — your roof and gutters collect thousands of gallons a year; landscaping decides where it all ends up
  • Trees managed, not just planted — limbs over the roof drop the debris that feeds moss and clogs gutters; we plant and prune with the roofline in mind
  • Beds that don't trap moisture — plantings against the house spaced and mulched so siding stays dry
  • Fire-wise zones where it counts — lean, clean, and green near the house for properties in wildfire-prone areas
  • Plants that fit the climate — chosen for soggy winters and bone-dry 86-degree summers, not the nursery catalog

We look at yards the way we look at roofs: where does the water go? Done right, landscaping protects the same house your roof does — and pairs naturally with a new fence, a deck, or moss removal while we're on site.

Landscaping FAQs

How does landscaping affect my foundation?

It's your foundation's first line of defense against 30+ inches of annual rain. Grading should slope away from the house, downspouts should discharge well clear of it, and beds shouldn't trap water against the siding. Get those right and the whole house stays drier, from crawl space to roofline.

What's fire-wise landscaping, and do I need it?

Keeping the area nearest your home lean, clean, and green: no flammable mulch or shrubs against the walls, limbs pruned up and away from the roof, low well-watered plants in the first few feet. After the 2020 Archie Creek Fire, homes near Douglas County's wildfire hazard areas benefit most — pair it with a Class A roof and some insurers take notice.

When's the best season for landscaping work?

Fall and early spring are prime planting windows — winter rains establish new plants for free. Grading, drainage, and hardscape work is best in the drier months so equipment doesn't churn wet ground into mud. We sequence the work to fit the calendar.

Do you do design and installation?

Yes, both — we walk the site, plan grading, drainage, and plantings, then do the installation ourselves. One crew from sketch to finished yard means the plan and the shovel work agree with each other. Afterward, our Home Care Membership keeps the roof-and-gutter side of the water story checked seasonally.

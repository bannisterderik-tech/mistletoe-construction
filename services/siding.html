Warning Signs

Is your siding still keeping the water out?

  • Warped, cracked, or cupping boards — moisture has been in and out of the material too many times
  • Soft spots — press near seams and bottom edges; give means rot
  • Paint that won't hold — peeling within a year or two usually means moisture pushing from behind
  • Gaps at seams, corners, and windows — every one is a door for wind-driven rain
  • Interior clues — bubbling paint, stains, or a musty smell on exterior walls

Questions

Siding FAQs

What are the signs siding needs replacement?

Warped, cracked, or cupping boards; soft spots that give when pressed; paint that peels within a year or two; gaps at seams and corners; and interior clues like bubbling paint or a musty smell on exterior walls. Any of those means water may already be behind the siding — and the fix gets more expensive the longer it soaks.

What's the best siding for our wet climate?

Fiber cement is the standout for 30+ inches of annual rain: no rot, excellent moisture resistance, and paint that lasts. Quality vinyl is the budget-friendly runner-up; wood works if you're committed to upkeep. Whichever you choose, the weather barrier and flashing behind it matter as much as the siding itself.

Can you match my existing siding?

Often, yes — we match profile, reveal, and texture as closely as current products allow, then color-match the finish. Honest caveat: decade-old weathered siding never perfectly matches fresh material on day one. Paint helps close the gap, and full-wall replacement reads cleaner than a mid-wall patch. We'll show you both options before you commit.

Does new siding help insulation?

Yes, meaningfully — mostly from what goes on with it. Residing is the ideal moment to add a proper weather barrier, seal air leaks, and install insulated sheathing under the new siding. The siding itself adds modest R-value; the tightened-up wall behind it is where the winter comfort comes from. Pair it with our winter-readiness guide for the full-house picture.

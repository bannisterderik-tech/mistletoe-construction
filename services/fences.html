Why Fences Fail Here

It's almost always the posts.

  • Wet rot at ground level — 30+ inches of rain a year sits right where the post meets the soil; untreated or badly set posts rot in a handful of winters
  • Wind leverage — a solid privacy fence is a sail; shallow posts lean after the first real valley storm
  • No drainage under the concrete — a post footing with no gravel base holds water like a cup
  • Wrong fasteners — non-galvanized hardware streaks, corrodes, and lets boards work loose
  • Gates hung on hope — the most-used part of the fence needs the stoutest post, not the leftover one

So that's where we spend the effort: pressure-treated or metal posts, set in concrete over gravel, deep enough for the wind load, with cedar or treated boards on top. It's the same philosophy as our roofing work — the parts you can't see decide how long the whole thing lasts. Pairing the fence with a new deck or landscaping? One crew, one schedule.

Questions

Fence building FAQs

What kind of fence lasts longest in this wet climate?

The longest-lasting fences pair rot-resistant materials with posts protected from standing water: cedar boards on pressure-treated posts, set in concrete with gravel underneath for drainage. Metal posts with wood boards last even longer. Whatever the material, a fence fails at the post line first — so that's where we spend the extra effort.

Should I talk to my neighbor first?

Yes — a five-minute conversation before we dig saves years of awkwardness after. Confirm where the line actually is, let your neighbor know the plan, and decide who gets the finished face. Oregon law generally expects adjoining owners to share responsibility for true boundary fences, so agree up front. We build to the line you establish.

How long does a typical fence take?

Plan on two to four working days for a typical residential fence: a day to set posts, a pause while concrete cures, then a day or two for rails, boards, and gates. Long field-fence runs take longer, and soggy mid-winter ground slows digging. Your written estimate includes a realistic schedule.

Do you repair fences too?

Both build and repair. We fix leaning posts, wind-damaged sections, sagging gates, and rotted boards — and tell you honestly when a fence is worth saving versus replacing. If you're on our Home Care Membership, the 10% repair discount applies here just like it does on roof repairs.

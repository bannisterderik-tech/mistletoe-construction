The Chain Reaction

A clogged gutter never damages just the gutter.

  • Fascia and soffit rot — overflowing water runs behind the gutter and soaks the wood it's mounted to
  • Roof edge damage — standing water wicks up under the first shingle course, and debris piles feed the moss that plagues every roof here
  • Foundation trouble — a winter of overflow dumps hundreds of gallons right at your footing: damp crawlspaces, settling, mold
  • Landscaping washout — the drip line carves trenches through beds and lawns
  • Ice and overflow at the worst time — a clog you could have cleared in October becomes a waterfall in a January storm

Every one of those repairs costs more than years of cleanings. That's the whole argument.

Questions

Gutter cleaning FAQs

How often should gutters be cleaned here?

Twice a year minimum for any home under trees: late fall after the oak leaves drop, and spring after winter storms shake down fir needles. Homes directly under Douglas firs often need a third visit — fir needles fall year-round and mat into a dense, water-blocking felt that leaves never form.

What happens if I just ignore them?

In our winters, clogged gutters overflow for months, not days. Water rots fascia boards, carves out landscaping, and dumps against the foundation — damp crawlspace, settling, mold. Cleaning costs a fraction of any one of those repairs. It's roof protection, not a chore.

Do you repair sagging gutters?

Yes — we re-secure sagging runs with new hangers, correct the slope so water actually reaches the downspouts, reseal leaking seams, and replace sections beyond saving. Sagging is usually a symptom of wet debris weight or failing fasteners, so we fix the cause, not just the droop.

When in the year should I schedule?

The key appointment is late fall — after most leaves are down, before the heaviest rain — so gutters enter the wet season clear. The second is spring, clearing what winter left behind. Members get this handled automatically with priority scheduling, so it never slips to January.

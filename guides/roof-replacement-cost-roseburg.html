Cost Guide

What a roof replacement really costs in Roseburg, Oregon.

Honest 2026 numbers from a Douglas County roofing crew: realistic local ranges, the seven factors that actually move the price, and how to read three competing bids without getting played.

By Mistletoe Construction Oregon CCB #255729 Updated July 2026 8 min read

Nobody enjoys pricing a roof. It's a big number, every contractor quotes it differently, and half the "cost guides" online are written for a national audience that doesn't tear off in the rain or deal with Douglas County's rules. So here's the straight version, from a crew that replaces roofs from Roseburg to Canyonville every season.

One promise up front: every number on this page is an estimate, not a quote. Roofs vary too much for anything else to be honest. The only real number is a written estimate for your actual roof — ours are free.

The realistic 2026 ranges for Douglas County

For a typical single-family home in the Roseburg area:

  • Architectural asphalt shingle replacement: most jobs land roughly between $10,000 and $25,000, full tear-off included. Smaller, simple ranch roofs can come in under that; large, steep, or cut-up roofs can go over.
  • Per square (100 sq ft): a reasonable planning figure for installed architectural asphalt here is roughly $450–$700 per square, covering tear-off, underlayment, flashing, and shingles. National averages run a bit lower, but they include a lot of overlay jobs and cheap-labor markets that don't apply here.
  • Metal roofing: plan on roughly 2–3 times the cost of asphalt for the same roof. It lasts 40–70 years and sheds our moss and debris far better, so the math isn't as lopsided as it looks — we walk through it in our metal vs. asphalt comparison.

If a bid comes in dramatically below these ranges, don't celebrate yet. Somebody is cutting something — usually underlayment, flashing, or insurance — and you'll meet the savings again later.

The 7 factors that actually move the price

FactorWhy it moves the number
1. Roof sizeThe biggest driver. Roofing is priced per square, so a 2,800 sq ft roof simply costs more than an 1,800 sq ft one — materials, labor, and disposal all scale with it.
2. Pitch (steepness)Steep roofs are slower and more dangerous to work. Anything a crew can't walk freely requires harnesses, staging, and more hours per square.
3. Tear-off layersTwo or three old layers means double or triple the tear-off labor and dump fees. You often can't know the layer count until someone looks.
4. Deck repairRotted or delaminated sheathing gets replaced by the sheet once the old roof is off. A good bid states the per-sheet price up front so there are no surprise change orders.
5. ComplexityValleys, dormers, skylights, chimneys, and dead-end walls all mean cutting, flashing, and detail work. A cut-up roof costs more per square than a simple gable of the same size.
6. Material tier3-tab vs. architectural vs. designer asphalt; standing-seam vs. through-fastened metal. Architectural shingles cost more than 3-tab but bring better wind ratings and roughly 10 more years of life — the right call in our climate.
7. AccessTight lots, landscaping to protect, long carries from the truck, or no room for the dumpster and delivery boom all add hours.

Why Douglas County's no-overlay rule affects your cost

In some parts of the country, roofers can nail a second layer of shingles over the first and skip the tear-off. Not here. Douglas County does not allow shingle-over-shingle overlays — reroofs require a full tear-off, and jobs over 100 square feet generally need a permit. Kick-out flashing at sidewalls is required too.

That makes local jobs cost more than a national overlay average, and it's worth every penny. Overlays trap heat, hide rot, add dead weight, and make the next replacement a double tear-off. The tear-off requirement means your deck actually gets inspected and repaired — the difference between a roof that lasts and one that just looks new from the street.

⚠ About "too good to be true" pricing

A bid that skips the permit, reuses old flashing, or quietly specs the thinnest underlayment can undercut an honest bid by thousands. You won't see the difference on day one. You'll see it in year six, from your living room, during an atmospheric river.

How to compare bids apples-to-apples

Three bids on the same roof can differ by $8,000 and all be "fair" — they're just pricing different jobs. Before you compare totals, make every bidder put these in writing:

  1. Exact scope. Full tear-off? How many layers assumed? Is the permit included and pulled by the contractor?
  2. The shingle, by name. Manufacturer and product line, not "30-year architectural." Lines differ meaningfully in wind rating and algae resistance — and in this climate, algae-resistant granules matter.
  3. Underlayment spec. Synthetic underlayment or felt? Ice-and-water membrane in the valleys and at penetrations? This is where cheap bids hide.
  4. Flashing plan. New step flashing, kick-outs, and pipe boots — or "reuse existing where possible"? Reused flashing is a leading cause of early leaks.
  5. Deck repair pricing. A stated per-sheet price for sheathing replacement, agreed before tear-off.
  6. Warranty terms. Manufacturer material warranty and the contractor's own workmanship warranty, both in writing.
  7. License verification. Look up every bidder's CCB number at search.ccb.state.or.us — about two minutes to see license status, bond, insurance, and complaint history. Our guide to verifying an Oregon contractor walks through it step by step. (Ours is CCB #255729 — check us too. Please.)

Red flags worth walking away from

  • A big deposit demand. Oregon roofers don't need half your money to order shingles. Large upfront deposits are how homeowners end up funding someone else's problems.
  • No written scope. A one-line quote — "tear off and reroof, $14,500" — tells you nothing about what you're buying. Everything above should be on paper.
  • Storm chasers. Out-of-area crews knock doors after every big blow. They're gone before the first callback. Ask where the company is based and who answers the phone in February.
  • No CCB number on the bid. It's required, and it's the two-minute check that filters out most trouble.
  • Pressure to sign today. A real price is still real next week.

Quick answers

How much does a roof replacement cost in Roseburg?

Most Douglas County asphalt replacements land roughly between $10,000 and $25,000 with a full tear-off; metal typically runs 2–3x that. Your roof's size, pitch, and condition set where you fall — get a written estimate for the real number.

Can I roof over my existing shingles here?

No — Douglas County requires full tear-off, no overlays, and reroofs over 100 sq ft generally need a permit. It costs more than an overlay would, and it's genuinely better for the roof.

What's a fair per-square price locally?

Roughly $450–$700 per square installed for architectural asphalt is a reasonable 2026 planning range here, with steep, complex, or multi-layer roofs pushing higher. Treat anything far below that range with suspicion.

What's the fastest trust check on a bidder?

Look up their CCB number at search.ccb.state.or.us. Two minutes shows license, bond, insurance, and complaint history. No CCB number on the bid is a walk-away.

Emergency Guide

Roof leak emergency: what to do in the first hour.

Water is coming through the ceiling and it's still raining. Here's exactly what to do, in order — including the one move that feels wrong but saves your ceiling — from a Douglas County crew that answers these calls.

By Mistletoe Construction Oregon CCB #255729 Updated July 2026 7 min read

A roof leak never announces itself on a sunny Tuesday. It shows up mid-storm, usually at night, usually somewhere inconvenient. The good news: the difference between a minor repair and a soaked, moldy mess mostly comes down to what you do in the first hour — and none of it requires going anywhere near the roof.

Work through this list in order. It's the same sequence we'd walk you through on the phone.

Step 1: Catch the water and protect what's under it

  • Bucket under the drip. Toss an old towel or a piece of cardboard in the bottom so the drip doesn't splash and drum all night.
  • Move what you can. Furniture, electronics, rugs, anything on the floor below. What won't move, cover with plastic sheeting, trash bags, or a shower curtain.
  • Kill power to the area if water is near fixtures. If water is running along a light fixture, ceiling fan, or outlet, switch off that circuit at the breaker panel before touching anything.

Step 2: If the ceiling is bulging, drain it — yes, on purpose

This is the step that surprises people. If you see a sagging bubble in the drywall or a paint blister filling with water, poke a small hole in the lowest point of the bulge with a screwdriver, with a bucket positioned underneath.

It feels like vandalism. It's actually damage control. Drywall holds a surprising amount of water before it fails, and while it holds, the water spreads sideways — soaking a wider and wider area of ceiling, insulation, and framing. When it finally lets go, it lets go all at once, and you're replacing a whole ceiling instead of patching a half-inch hole. A controlled drain releases the water where you decided, into a bucket, and limits the wet area. One small hole is the cheapest repair on this entire page.

Step 3: Photograph everything, now

Before you clean anything up, get your phone out. Shoot the active drip, the ceiling stain, the bulge (before and after draining), the bucket, wet belongings, and — if the leak followed a storm — any debris in the yard, downed limbs, or shingles on the ground. Take video too; video with sound of active dripping is compelling evidence.

If this turns into an insurance claim, timestamped photos of active damage are worth far more than photos of a dried stain a week later. Our storm damage insurance guide covers the full claims process, but the short version is: document first, clean up second.

Step 4: Play detective in the attic

If you have safe attic access, take a flashlight up and look for the entry point — carefully, stepping only on framing, never on the drywall between joists. Clues to look for:

  • Drip trails and shine. Water rarely drops straight down. It runs along rafters and sheathing, sometimes 10+ feet from the actual hole, before finding a low point. Follow the wet streak uphill.
  • Daylight. Turn the flashlight off. Pinpricks of light through the roof deck mark punctures or missing shingles.
  • Wet insulation. A dark, compressed patch of insulation marks where water has been landing — often directly below the ceiling stain, but trace upward from there.
  • Location hints. Leaks cluster around penetrations: chimneys, plumbing vent boots, skylights, and valleys. If the wet trail leads to one of these, tell your roofer — it speeds up the fix.

Note what you find, photograph it, and come back down. You're gathering intel, not fixing anything up there.

⚠ Safety absolutes

Do not get on a wet roof. Ever. A rain-slick roof is a fall injury waiting to happen, and Douglas County roofs are wet from October to May. No ladder work in wind or rain, no walking the roof in the dark, no leaning out of upstairs windows with a tarp. Nothing on your roof tonight is worth an ER visit — the water damage is fixable.

Step 5: Tarp or wait?

Emergency tarping makes sense when there's visible damage (missing shingles, a punctured deck, a fallen limb) and more rain coming — which, from October through May here, is a safe bet. But tarping is rooftop work in exactly the conditions we just told you to stay out of. It's a job for a crew with fall protection, and it's one we do.

Waiting is reasonable when the leak is a slow seep, the forecast gives a dry window, and a roofer can get to you within a day or two. A cup of water in a bucket overnight is annoying, not catastrophic. If you're a Home Care Member, this decision is easy: emergency tarping of up to 100 sq ft of storm damage is included per incident, and members get priority scheduling — you call, we handle it.

Step 6: Make the calls — in this order

  1. A licensed local roofer first. Call or text us at (541) 670-5005. A roofer stops the active damage, finds the real source, and gives you a written assessment — which is exactly what an insurance adjuster will want to see anyway. Most leaks turn out to be targeted roof repairs, not replacements.
  2. Your insurer second, if a storm caused it. Wind, fallen limbs, and hail are typically covered perils; slow deterioration and deferred maintenance typically aren't. If the leak followed a storm, open a claim after you have photos and a roofer's take, so you're reporting facts instead of guesses. The insurance claims guide walks through what Oregon adjusters look for.

One more honest note: clogged gutters cause a surprising share of "roof leaks" here — water backs up over the gutter lip and finds its way behind fascia and under the first shingle course. If your gutters were overflowing during the storm, mention it; sometimes the fix is a gutter cleaning, not roof surgery. And if the leak hit during a cold snap, ice and frozen debris play their own games — our winter roofing guide covers that scenario.

After the storm: what a real fix looks like

A proper follow-up isn't just smearing sealant on the suspect spot. Expect a roofer to trace the leak from the inside evidence to the outside source, check the surrounding flashing and boots (failures travel in groups), photograph the damage before and after, and give you a written scope. If anyone quotes you a full replacement over the phone without getting on the roof, get a second opinion.

Quick answers

Should I really poke a hole in a bulging ceiling?

Yes. A small controlled hole at the low point, bucket underneath, releases trapped water where you choose. Left alone, the water spreads until a much larger section of drywall collapses at once.

Who do I call first?

A licensed local roofer — (541) 670-5005 in Douglas County — to stop and document the damage. Then your insurer, if a storm caused it, once you have photos and an assessment.

Can I find the leak from the roof myself?

Not while it's wet. Investigate from the attic instead: follow drip trails uphill, look for daylight through the deck, and note wet insulation. Leave the rooftop to a crew with fall protection.

Does a leak mean I need a new roof?

Usually not. Most leaks trace to one failed component — a boot, flashing, or a few shingles — and a targeted repair fixes them. Replacement is for end-of-life roofs leaking in multiple places.

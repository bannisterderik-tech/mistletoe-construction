Comparison Guide

Metal roof vs asphalt shingles in western Oregon: an honest comparison.

We install both, so we have no horse in this race. Here's how the two actually stack up in a climate that rains 130 days a year and grows moss like a crop — including the math most comparisons skip.

By Mistletoe Construction Oregon CCB #255729 Updated July 2026 8 min read

Ask three roofers "metal or asphalt?" and you'll usually learn more about what they sell than what you need. We install metal roofing and asphalt replacements in roughly equal seriousness, so our only stake here is you not regretting the choice in ten years. Short version: both are good roofs. They're good at different things, and western Oregon's climate changes the usual calculus.

The rain-noise myth, retired

The number one question we get about metal: "won't it be loud when it rains?" It's a fair worry — everyone's stood in a barn or under a carport during a downpour and heard the drumming. But that's bare metal over open framing.

A residential metal roof goes over solid sheathing, underlayment, and above an insulated attic. All that mass soaks up the sound. In practice it lands within a few decibels of asphalt, and most homeowners honestly can't tell the difference from the living room. In a place with 130 rain days a year, if metal roofs were loud, western Oregon would have noticed by now.

The moss angle — where Oregon tilts the table

Generic national comparisons underweight the single biggest local factor: the Umpqua Valley is world-class moss habitat, and moss is the number one killer of asphalt roofs here.

Asphalt's granular texture gives moss rhizoids a perfect grip; an unmanaged mat can take a decade off a shingle roof's life. Metal's smooth painted surface offers nothing to hold onto, and fir needles slide off instead of composting in the valleys. Choosing metal effectively deletes a maintenance line item that asphalt owners here carry for the life of the roof — typically $250–$600 per professional treatment cycle, or the discipline of the prevention calendar in our moss guide.

That said, asphalt fights back better than it used to: algae-resistant shingles with copper-infused granules are standard in quality architectural lines now, and zinc strips plus annual fall treatment keep a comp roof clean if you actually do it. The honest framing: metal solves moss with money; asphalt solves it with maintenance.

When asphalt is genuinely the right answer

Metal evangelists skip this part. We won't:

  • The budget is the budget. If $15,000 is what the project can bear, a quality architectural asphalt roof — Class A, algae-resistant, properly flashed — is a genuinely good roof, not a consolation prize. A stretched budget that shortchanges flashing details to afford metal is a worse roof than well-executed asphalt.
  • Complex rooflines. Lots of valleys, dormers, hips, and penetrations multiply metal's fabrication and labor cost, and every extra seam is a detail to get right. Asphalt handles cut-up roofs gracefully and affordably.
  • Matching the neighborhood. On some streets a metal roof looks like a statement you didn't mean to make. Resale-wise, the "expected" roof done well beats the standout roof done arguably.
  • Shorter horizon. Selling within 5–10 years? You'll pay metal's full premium and hand most of its lifespan to the next owner.

The cost-per-year math

Here's the frame that actually settles most decisions. Take a hypothetical Douglas County roof (all numbers estimates — your written estimate is the real math):

  • Asphalt: say $16,000, lasting ~22 years with maintenance → roughly $730/year, plus moss treatments along the way.
  • Metal: say $38,000, lasting ~50 years → roughly $760/year, with minimal maintenance.

Per year of service, they're nearly the same roof. The real difference is when you pay: asphalt spreads the cost across two or three replacements (at whatever prices those future decades bring); metal front-loads it once. If you're in your forever home, metal's one-and-done math is compelling. If this is a five-year house, asphalt almost always wins.

Our take, since you asked

Staying 15+ years, simple roofline, trees nearby? Metal earns its premium here more than in most climates — mostly because of moss. Tighter budget, complex roof, or a shorter horizon? Quick answers

Is metal worth it here?

Often yes if you're staying long-term: 2–3x the upfront cost, but 40–70 years of life, near-zero moss, and Class A fire rating. Cost-per-year, it roughly matches asphalt.

Are metal roofs loud in the rain?

Not on a house. Solid sheathing, underlayment, and attic insulation absorb the sound to within a few decibels of asphalt. The drumming you remember was a barn.

Does moss grow on metal?

Barely — the smooth surface gives rhizoids no grip and sheds debris. That's metal's biggest advantage specifically in the Umpqua Valley.

When is asphalt the right call?

Fixed budget, complex roofline, neighborhood fit, or a shorter ownership horizon. A well-installed Class A architectural asphalt roof is a genuinely good roof, not a compromise.

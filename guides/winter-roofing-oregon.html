Seasonal Guide

Can you replace a roof in an Oregon winter? Yes — here's how.

"Call back in June" is not a plan when your ceiling is dripping in January. How crews actually roof between storms, what cold does to shingles, and the honest cases where waiting really is the right call.

By Mistletoe Construction Oregon CCB #255729 Updated July 2026 7 min read

Every winter we take calls from Douglas County homeowners who assume roofing season ended in October — some of them standing under an active leak, planning to live with buckets until summer. So let's clear this up: roofs get replaced in western Oregon all winter long. It takes more planning, a disciplined crew, and sometimes a little patience with the forecast. But "impossible" it is not — and waiting out a wet season with a failing roof is usually the most expensive option on the table.

The weather windows are real

Roseburg's wet season runs October through May, with around 130 rain days a year — but rain days aren't a solid wall of water. Pacific storm systems roll through in pulses, and between them come the dry windows: two, three, sometimes five clear days at a stretch, even in the dead of winter.

A typical Douglas County home can be torn off and dried-in — underlayment down, roof watertight — in a single day per section. That means a two- or three-day window is genuinely enough to replace most roofs safely. The job isn't scheduled like a summer job ("we start Tuesday"); it's scheduled like a winter job ("we start at the front edge of the next dry window, and we watch the forecast like hawks"). Modern forecasting makes those windows predictable enough to plan around.

What cold actually does to shingles

The real technical question isn't rain — it's temperature. Asphalt shingles have a factory-applied self-seal strip that bonds each shingle to the one below it, and that adhesive is heat-activated. The industry guideline, echoed by major manufacturers and the Asphalt Roofing Manufacturers Association, is that self-sealing wants ambient temperatures of roughly 40°F and above, plus some sun on the roof.

Below that, shingles don't seal on their own right away — and cold shingles get stiffer and need more careful handling. None of that stops a winter install; it changes the procedure:

  • Hand-sealing. In cold conditions, crews hand-seal shingles with manufacturer-approved roofing cement — especially along rakes, eaves, ridges, and wind-exposed planes — so the roof is wind-resistant immediately instead of waiting for spring sun.
  • Warm storage. Bundles stored warm and brought up in batches stay flexible and nail cleanly.
  • Nailing care. Cold, stiff shingles punish sloppy gun work; pressure gets checked so nails seat flush without blowing through.

Then, when the first warm spell arrives, the factory strips activate and finish the bond on their own. Roseburg winters are mild by roofing standards — our January days often sit in the 40s — so plenty of winter days here don't even require the cold-weather playbook.

The one non-negotiable rule

Never open more roof than you can dry-in the same day. Every winter horror story you've heard — the tarp that blew off, the flooded living room — starts with a crew that tore off more than the day's weather could cover. Section-by-section tear-off with same-day underlayment is what separates winter pros from summer-only outfits.

The upside nobody mentions: winter scheduling

Here's the quiet advantage. Roofing demand in Douglas County peaks June through September, when everyone who nursed a roof through the wet season calls at once — and calendars book out weeks. In winter, those same calendars open up.

Practically, that means a winter roof replacement can often start within days of a signed estimate instead of waiting behind a summer backlog, with more crew attention and more scheduling flexibility to hit the best weather window. Materials are the same, the workmanship standard is the same, the warranty is the same. If your roof is due anyway, winter isn't the consolation slot — it's frequently the shortest line.

What a pro crew does differently in winter

  • Forecast-driven scheduling — the start date floats to the front of a confirmed dry window, not a date picked three weeks ago.
  • Section-by-section staging — tear-off sized to what can be fully dried-in before dark, every day.
  • Same-day dry-in, always — synthetic underlayment down and sealed before the crew leaves, so a surprise shower hits underlayment, not deck.
  • Tarps staged on the roof, not in the truck — if a cell pops up on radar, cover goes on in minutes.
  • Hand-sealing and warm material handling — as above, so the roof is storm-ready the day it's finished.
  • Shorter, sharper work days — winter daylight is 9 to 4:30; honest crews plan around it rather than nailing by headlamp.

When we'll tell you to wait

Honesty clause: winter replacement isn't always the right call, and we'll say so when it isn't.

  • The roof is fine. If it's aging but sound and dry, there's zero reason to force a January install. We'll put it on the spring or summer calendar and check it after big storms in the meantime.
  • A hard-freeze or snow stretch is parked overhead. The occasional week of ice or snow in the forecast means we push the start, not push through it.
  • Steep, complex roofs in marginal conditions. Some roofs need bigger windows to do safely and correctly. If yours is one, we'll tell you the window we need instead of gambling with your deck — or your crew.
  • A repair honestly bridges you. Sometimes a few hundred dollars of targeted roof repair gets a roof through to a summer replacement in fine shape. If that's the truth, that's the recommendation — even though it's the smaller ticket.

Leaking now? The triage sequence

If water is coming in this week, the order of operations is: stop the water today, fix the roof in the next window. An emergency tarp is not a defeat — it's the correct bridge. Professionally installed, a tarp keeps a damaged section dry for weeks while the estimate, materials, and weather line up.

Quick answers

Can a roof really be replaced here in winter?

Yes. Dry windows of two to five days occur between storm systems all winter, and a disciplined crew tears off only what it can dry-in the same day. It's planned differently, not skipped.

Do shingles seal below 40°F?

Not on their own — so crews hand-seal with approved roofing cement in cold or windy conditions, and the factory strips finish bonding when warm weather returns. Manufacturers publish these cold-weather procedures.

Is winter actually easier to book?

Usually, yes. Summer backlogs vanish, so winter jobs often start within days of a signed estimate — same materials, same standard, shorter line.

Should a January leak wait for July?

No. Tarp or repair it now, then do the full fix in the next window. Six months of wet-season water turns small problems into big ones.

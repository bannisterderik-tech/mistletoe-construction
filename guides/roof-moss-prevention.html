Maintenance Guide

Moss on your roof: remove it, and keep it off.

The Umpqua Valley is world-class moss habitat. Here's why it's eating your shingles, why pressure washing makes it worse, and what actually works — from a crew that deals with it every week.

By Mistletoe Construction Oregon CCB #255729 Updated July 2026 7 min read

Drive any street in Roseburg, Riddle, or Myrtle Creek in February and you'll see it: green velvet creeping up the north slope of half the roofs on the block. Moss is so normal here that most homeowners ignore it — until a ceiling stain shows up.

Here's the thing: moss is not cosmetic. It's one of the most common reasons western Oregon roofs die a decade early. The good news is that dealing with it correctly is neither hard nor expensive — if you do it before the moss wins.

Why the Umpqua Valley grows moss so well

Moss needs three things: moisture, shade, and something to grip. Douglas County delivers all three in abundance:

  • ~130 rainy days a year. Roseburg picks up most of its 30–33 inches of annual rain between October and May. A shaded roof plane may not fully dry for weeks at a time in winter.
  • Tree canopy. The oaks and Douglas-firs that make our neighborhoods beautiful also cast the shade moss loves — and drop the needle-and-leaf litter that holds moisture and feeds growth.
  • Asphalt shingle texture. The granular surface of a composition roof is a perfect anchor. Moss rhizoids work between granules and under shingle edges.

North- and east-facing planes get hit first because they see the least sun. If your neighbor's south slope is clean but their north slope is green, that's why.

What moss actually does to a roof

  • It lifts shingle edges. As moss mats thicken, they physically pry up the edges of shingles — breaking the sealed bond that keeps wind-driven rain out.
  • It holds water against the roof. A moss mat works like a wet sponge strapped to your shingles. That constant moisture wicks into nail holes and seams and accelerates granule loss.
  • It dams water flow. Thick moss in valleys and along eaves interrupts drainage, pushing water sideways under shingles instead of down into gutters.
  • It shortens roof life. A roof that should last 25 years can fail in 15 under an unmanaged moss load — and moss damage is often excluded from warranty coverage as a maintenance issue.

⚠ The pressure-washer mistake

Every year we inspect roofs ruined not by moss but by the cure. Never pressure wash an asphalt roof. It blasts off the protective granules (the roof's UV armor), drives water under shingles, and can void your manufacturer warranty. If a "roof cleaner" shows up with a pressure washer, send them home.

The right way to remove moss

  1. Wait for dry moss. Late summer is ideal — drought-stressed moss releases its grip and brushes away with far less shingle damage.
  2. Brush, don't blast. A stiff (not wire) brush or broom, working down-slope so you don't lift shingle edges, removes the bulk of the mat.
  3. Treat what remains. A moss-killing treatment (typically zinc- or potassium-salt based) applied to the roof kills the rhizoids the brush can't reach. Then Oregon's rain does the rinsing over the following weeks.
  4. Clear the gutters after. Dead moss sheds for a while — gutters need a follow-up clean so the debris doesn't create the next problem.

Walking a wet or mossy roof is genuinely dangerous, and steep pitches shouldn't be walked at all. This is honest-to-goodness "worth paying a pro" territory — a professional moss removal in the Roseburg area typically runs a few hundred dollars, which is cheap insurance on a $15,000 roof.

A realistic moss calendar for Douglas County

  • Aug–Sep: Remove dry moss, apply preventive treatment, trim limbs.
  • Oct–Nov: Clean gutters after leaf drop; verify the roof is shedding water cleanly going into the wet season.
  • Feb–Mar: Quick visual check from the ground — winter is when moss does its growing, and catching a green fuzz early beats scraping a mat later.

Quick answers

Can I just ignore a little moss?

A light green fuzz is your early-warning window — treatment now is cheap and easy. An established mat means shingle damage is already underway.

Do zinc strips really work?

Yes, as prevention on a clean roof, for about 10–15 feet below the strip. They will not clear an existing infestation.

What does professional removal cost here?

Most Roseburg-area jobs land roughly between $250 and $600 depending on size, pitch, and severity. Heavy multi-treatment jobs run more.

Best time of year?

Late summer to early fall — weak moss, dry roof, and prevention in place before the rains return.

Warranty Guide

Roof warranties, decoded: what's actually covered

"Lifetime warranty" is doing a lot of work on that shingle wrapper. Here's the honest version — the two warranties every roof has, what the fine print takes back, and the everyday things that quietly void coverage.

By Mistletoe Construction Oregon CCB #255729 Updated July 2026 7 min read

Warranties come up in nearly every estimate conversation we have, usually as a single question: "Is it covered?" The honest answer is that a roof carries two separate warranties from two separate parties, and the gap between them is where homeowners get burned. Let's close that gap.

The two warranties every roof has

  • The manufacturer's material warranty covers the shingles themselves — factory defects, premature granule loss caused by the product, that kind of thing. It comes from the shingle maker, not your roofer, and it covers materials.
  • The contractor's workmanship warranty covers the installation — flashing details, nailing pattern, sealing, valleys, everything human hands did on your roof. It comes from the company that installed it, in writing, or it doesn't exist.

Here's the part that matters: most roof problems in the first decade are installation problems. A leak at a chimney is almost never a defective shingle; it's a flashing detail. The manufacturer's warranty — the impressive one with the big number — covers none of that. The modest-sounding workmanship warranty is the one that gets used. When you're interviewing roofers, ask about both separately; it's question eight in our twelve questions guide.

What "lifetime" actually means

"Lifetime" shingle warranties are real, but they're not what the word suggests. The typical structure:

  • An initial full-coverage period — commonly around the first decade — where a legitimate material defect gets meaningful replacement coverage.
  • Prorating after that. The payout shrinks every year based on the roof's age. A material defect discovered in year 22 of a "lifetime" roof may be worth a fraction of replacement cost — and typically materials only. Labor, tear-off, and disposal, which are most of a reroof bill, are usually on you.
  • "Lifetime" means the original owner's tenure, with transfer rules if the house sells (more below).

None of this makes the warranty worthless. It makes it limited — a defect backstop, not a maintenance plan or a second roof in waiting. Plan your roof's economics around its real lifespan in this climate (our lifespan guide has the honest numbers), not around the wrapper.

What voids a roof warranty

This is the section worth bookmarking, because the common warranty-killers in western Oregon are all everyday choices:

Warranty killerWhy
Pressure washingStrips the protective granules; manufacturers treat it as abuse, not maintenance. The number-one self-inflicted wound around here — our moss guide covers the safe alternative.
Inadequate attic ventilationMost manufacturers require ventilation to spec and can deny claims for heat- and moisture-related failure without it. A roof can be voided from the attic without anyone touching a shingle.
Layover installsShingles over old shingles trap heat and violate most warranty terms. Douglas County prohibits them anyway — full tear-off required — but older layover roofs are out there, uncovered.
Improper installationWrong nailing, wrong fasteners, off-spec details can void material coverage — the manufacturer's warranty assumes the product was installed to their instructions.
Unapproved repairs & coatingsMystery sealants and non-approved patch jobs give an adjuster an easy exit. Keep repairs professional and documented.
Neglected maintenanceEstablished moss, chronically clogged gutters, debris dams — damage from neglect is routinely excluded as a maintenance failure.

Notice the pattern: the warranty assumes a properly installed, properly maintained roof. Miss either half and the paper protection evaporates. Registration and transfer: the boring steps that matter

  • Registration. Many enhanced warranty terms require the warranty to be registered after installation, within a set window. Ask your contractor point-blank who registers it and get confirmation it happened. This step gets skipped constantly, and nobody notices until there's a claim.
  • Keep the paperwork. The contract, the itemized estimate showing what was installed (here's how to read one), the registration confirmation, and your maintenance records. A warranty claim is a documentation exercise.
  • Transfer at sale. Many manufacturer warranties allow one transfer to the next owner, usually within a set window after closing and sometimes at reduced coverage. If you're selling, a registered, transferable roof warranty is a genuine selling point; if you're buying, ask for it.

The one-paragraph version

Two warranties: manufacturer covers the shingle, contractor covers the install. "Lifetime" prorates. Pressure washing, bad ventilation, layovers, and neglect void coverage. Register it, keep the paperwork, transfer it when you sell. And the best warranty is a roof installed well enough that you never open the folder.

Quick answers

What's the difference between a manufacturer warranty and a workmanship warranty?

The manufacturer's warranty covers the shingles — factory defects in the material. The workmanship warranty comes from your contractor and covers the installation. Most first-decade roof problems are installation problems, which the manufacturer won't cover — so a contractor with no written workmanship warranty leaves your likeliest failure mode uninsured.

Does a "lifetime" shingle warranty really mean forever?

No. Full replacement coverage typically applies only during an initial period of roughly the first decade; after that it prorates down each year and usually covers materials only — not the labor and disposal that make up most of a reroof's cost. Read the prorating schedule.

What can void a roof warranty?

The usual suspects here: pressure washing, inadequate attic ventilation, layover installs over old shingles, unapproved repairs or coatings, neglected maintenance like established moss — and improper installation, which can void even the material coverage.

Do roof warranties transfer when I sell my house?

Often yes, but rarely automatically — many allow one transfer within a set window after the sale, sometimes at reduced coverage, and the warranty must have been registered originally. Buying a house with a newish roof? Ask for the registration and installer's name during the sale.

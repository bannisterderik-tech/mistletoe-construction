Wildfire Guide

Wildfire-resistant roofing in Douglas County: Class A roofs, Oregon rules & insurance.

After Archie Creek, "fire-resistant roof" stopped being an abstraction here. What Class A actually means, how embers really get in, what the code requires, and what your insurance company wants to see.

By Mistletoe Construction Oregon CCB #255729 Updated July 2026 8 min read

In September 2020, the Archie Creek Fire tore down the North Umpqua corridor near Glide and burned over a hundred homes. If you live anywhere in Douglas County, you don't need a lecture on wildfire risk — you remember the smoke. What most homeowners don't have is a clear picture of what actually protects a house, what the rules now require, and what their insurer is quietly grading them on.

Here's the part that surprises people: most homes lost to wildfire aren't touched by the flame front at all. They're ignited by wind-blown embers, often landing well ahead of the fire. And the single largest ember target on your property is your roof. That's why roofing is where wildfire hardening starts.

What "Class A" actually means

Roof coverings are fire-rated Class A, B, or C based on standardized burn tests — Class A is the top rating, meaning the assembly resists severe fire exposure, including burning brands sitting directly on the surface. Three Class A options dominate around here:

  • Fiberglass-based asphalt composition shingles. The workhorse. Nearly all quality architectural asphalt shingles sold today carry a Class A assembly rating — if your comp roof is reasonably modern, you may already have one. This is the affordable path to Class A, and at replacement time it costs nothing extra to confirm you're getting it.
  • Metal roofing. Steel doesn't burn, and as part of a rated assembly over the right underlayment, metal roofs earn Class A while also shedding the needles and debris that embers love to ignite. It's the belt-and-suspenders choice for homes up the North Umpqua, in Glide, or backed up against timber.
  • Concrete or clay tile. Also Class A, though less common in Douglas County — heavy, and not every structure is framed for it.

⚠ "Assembly" is the key word

The Class A rating usually belongs to the whole system — covering plus underlayment over the deck — not the top layer alone. A great shingle over the wrong underlayment may not make the rating. This is one of those details a bid should spell out; ask your roofer which rated assembly they're installing.

The rules: Oregon R327 and the county shake ban

Two layers of regulation matter locally:

  • Oregon's R327 wildfire hazard code. Oregon's residential code includes wildfire hazard mitigation provisions (Section R327) that favor Class A roof assemblies and ember-resistant construction details in designated hazard areas — vents, roof edges, and the roof covering itself.
  • Douglas County's Wildfire Hazard Overlay District. In the county's mapped higher-risk areas, wood shake roofs are prohibited. If you've got an old shake roof in or near the overlay district, replacement won't be shakes-for-shakes — and honestly, it shouldn't be anywhere in this county. A dry cedar roof in August is kindling with a mortgage.

Remember Douglas County's other reroof rules apply too: full tear-off (no overlays) and a permit for jobs over 100 square feet. Details on how that affects budget are in our roof replacement cost guide.

How embers actually get in (and how to shut the doors)

A Class A covering solves the "ember lands on the roof surface" problem. But embers are small, wind-driven, and patient — they find the gaps. The usual entry points:

Entry pointThe fix
Gutters full of fir needlesThe classic Douglas County failure: an ember lands in a gutter packed with dry needles, ignites, and the fire climbs into the roof edge. Regular gutter cleaning — or gutter guards that keep needles out in the first place — closes this door.
Attic, soffit & crawlspace ventsOpen vents inhale embers straight into the driest space in the house. Fine metal mesh (1/8") or ember-resistant vents block entry while preserving airflow.
Lifted or damaged shinglesWind-lifted edges and failed sealant give embers a path under the covering. Keeping the roof sealed and repaired is fire defense, not just leak defense.
SkylightsDebris collects against skylight curbs, and older plastic domes are vulnerable. Keep curbs clear; choose tempered-glass units at replacement.
Debris on the roof itselfNeedle drifts in valleys and behind chimneys are ember fuel sitting on top of your Class A roof. Seasonal clearing matters as much as the material.

Notice the theme: half of ember defense is maintenance, not materials. A Class A roof buried in dry needles has given back much of its advantage. That seasonal clearing is exactly what our Home Care Membership handles — annual inspection, debris checks after storms, and a photo report you can keep on file.

Defensible space, briefly

We're roofers, not wildland crews, so we'll keep this short: the roof works with the ground around it. The basics that matter most — keep the first 5 feet around the house free of bark mulch, dry plants, and stacked firewood; limb up trees near the structure; don't let branches overhang the roof (they drop the very needle litter embers ignite — and feed the moss problem the rest of the year, so trimming pays twice). Oregon State Extension and your local fire district publish full defensible-space guidance worth reading.

The insurance angle: discounts, pressure, and paperwork

Since the 2020 fires, wildfire has reshaped Oregon homeowners insurance. In practice, Douglas County homeowners are seeing three things:

  • Carrots. Some insurers offer discounts or preferred rates for Class A roofs and documented home-hardening.
  • Sticks. In higher-risk areas, insurers increasingly ask about roof material and age at renewal — and nonrenewal pressure is real for homes with old shake roofs or visibly deferred maintenance.
  • Paperwork wins. The homeowners who benefit are the ones who can prove it. Keep the contractor invoice naming the exact product line, the permit record, photos of the finished roof, and any assembly documentation — then send copies to your agent and ask specifically about wildfire-related credits.

Every replacement we do comes with that documentation. It's a small thing that can matter a lot at renewal time.

Quick answers

What is a Class A roof?

The highest fire rating for a roof assembly — it withstands severe fire-test exposure including burning brands on the surface. Fiberglass asphalt comp, metal (over the right underlayment), and tile all get there.

Are wood shakes allowed here?

Not in Douglas County's Wildfire Hazard Overlay District, where they're prohibited — and Oregon's R327 code pushes hazard-area roofs toward Class A assemblies generally. Replace old shakes with comp, metal, or tile.

Will a Class A roof help my insurance?

Increasingly yes — as a discount, or as the thing that keeps you insurable. Document the product line, permit, and photos, and send them to your agent.

What's the cheapest meaningful upgrade?

Clean gutters and mesh-screened vents. Embers ignite dry needles in gutters and slip through open vents far more often than they defeat a modern Class A covering.

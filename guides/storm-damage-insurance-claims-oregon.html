Storm damage roof insurance claims in Oregon: a homeowner's playbook

A limb through the roof or a slope stripped by wind is stressful enough. Here's the calm, step-by-step version — what to document, what your policy actually says, how the claim really goes, and when filing isn't worth it.

By Mistletoe Construction Oregon CCB #255729 Updated July 2026 9 min read

Douglas County storms don't usually make national news, but a Pacific front driving 50-mph gusts up the Umpqua Valley will strip shingles, drop fir limbs, and peel back ridge caps just fine. When it happens to your house, the order you do things in matters — both for keeping water out and for how your insurance claim goes.

This is the sequence we walk homeowners through. None of it is legal or insurance advice — your policy and your insurer's instructions govern — but it's the practical playbook from a crew that ends up on a lot of these roofs.

Step 1: Document everything, before you touch anything

Your claim is only as strong as your evidence, and evidence is easiest to gather in the first hours:

  • Photos and video, wide and close. From the ground and from windows — the damaged area, the whole roof plane, shingles or debris in the yard, the limb that did it. Don't climb a storm-damaged roof; ground and zoom is fine.
  • Interior damage too. Ceiling stains, wet insulation, drips — with something for scale, and timestamps.
  • Date, time, and the weather. Note when the storm hit and what it was (wind, hail, falling tree). Screenshot a local weather report or wind gust reading for that day — adjusters check storm data, and your record should match.
  • Save damaged material. Keep a few of the torn shingles. Physical evidence of wind damage is persuasive.

Step 2: Stop the bleeding — you're required to

Nearly every homeowner's policy includes a duty to mitigate: after a loss, you must take reasonable steps to prevent further damage. Water pouring through an unprotected hole for a week is damage the insurer can argue you allowed — and may not pay for.

In practice, mitigation means emergency tarping — a properly anchored tarp over the breach until permanent repairs happen. Keep the receipt; reasonable mitigation costs are typically claimable. It does not mean making permanent repairs before the adjuster sees the damage.

Step 3: Actually read your policy

Before you file, spend twenty minutes with your declarations page. Three things determine how this goes:

What to findWhy it matters
RCV vs ACV on the roofRCV (replacement cost) pays today's cost to replace, usually releasing depreciation after work completes. ACV (actual cash value) subtracts depreciation permanently — on an older roof, that can be half the bill or less. Insurers increasingly move aging roofs to ACV or payment schedules at renewal; many homeowners don't notice until claim time.
Covered perilsWind and hail are standard covered perils; sudden falling-object damage (that fir limb) generally is too. What's not covered: wear and tear, neglected maintenance, and long-term moss or leak damage. A storm claim on a roof that was already failing gets contested.
Your deductible(s)Note whether you have a separate, higher wind/hail deductible — some policies do, sometimes a percentage of dwelling coverage rather than a flat dollar figure. This number drives the "is it worth filing" math below.

Step 4: The claims sequence, start to finish

  1. Report the claim. Call your insurer or use their app, promptly — policies require timely notice. Give the facts and your documentation; don't speculate about cause or pre-existing condition.
  2. Adjuster inspection. The insurer sends an adjuster to inspect and scope the damage. It's smart to have your own roofing contractor present or to have their inspection report in hand — adjusters see the roof once; a roofer can point out damage that's easy to miss from a quick walk.
  3. Contractor estimate. Get a detailed written estimate from a licensed local contractor for the real cost of proper repair or replacement. This is your reality check against the insurer's scope.
  4. Compare scopes and supplement. If the adjuster's scope misses items — code-required tear-off, kick-out flashing, matching issues, hidden deck damage found mid-job — your contractor submits a supplement with documentation. Supplements are a normal, expected part of the process, not a fight.
  5. Work, then depreciation. On RCV policies you typically get an initial ACV payment, complete the work, submit the final invoice, and receive the withheld depreciation. Keep every document.

Timelines: report promptly (days, not months — and note that policies set deadlines for filing and for completing repairs to recover depreciation). Adjuster visits usually happen within days to a couple of weeks, longer after a widespread storm. The whole cycle commonly runs several weeks to a few months. Your insurer's letters state the specific deadlines that bind you — read them.

How a good contractor helps — and how to spot a storm chaser

A legitimate local contractor documents damage honestly, writes a scope your adjuster can verify, meets the adjuster on site, and supplements with evidence when the scope is short. What they don't do is inflate claims or play games with your deductible.

After every big storm, out-of-area "storm chasers" sweep through. Red flags, in rough order of how fast you should close the door:

  • Door-knocking right after the storm, often with an out-of-state plate and a "we're doing your neighbor's roof" line.
  • "We'll waive your deductible." This is the big one — deductible waiving or rebating means misrepresenting the claim to your insurer, and that's insurance fraud. Illegal, full stop, and it tells you exactly how they operate.
  • Pressure to sign now — an "assignment of benefits" or contingency agreement shoved at you on the porch before any adjuster has seen the roof.
  • No local address, no CCB number on the paperwork. Every legitimate Oregon contractor has both. Verify any contractor in two minutes at the CCB — if they're not licensed in Oregon, the conversation is over.
  • They "find" damage you can't see and discourage you from getting a second opinion.

The chaser business model is volume: file everything, install fast and thin, and be three states away when the workmanship problems show up. Your warranty is worth exactly as much as the odds of that truck ever coming back.

When a claim ISN'T worth filing (honest math)

Here's the part a roofer chasing insurance work won't tell you: sometimes filing is a bad deal for you. Run the numbers first:

  • Repair below or near the deductible = don't file. A $1,200 repair against a $2,500 deductible pays you $0 and still logs a claim on your CLUE report.
  • Claims history follows you. Filed claims can affect your premiums for years and are visible to insurers if you shop policies. One modest claim can cost more in premium creep than it paid out.
  • ACV on an old roof changes everything. If your 18-year-old roof is on ACV, a "totaled" roof payout may be a fraction of replacement cost. Sometimes the smarter move is a paid repair now and a planned replacement on your own terms — see our honest take on what replacement actually costs around Roseburg.
  • Rule of thumb: file when damage clearly and substantially exceeds your deductible; pay out of pocket when it doesn't; and get an honest repair estimate before deciding, not after.

Quick answers

What do I do first after storm damage?

Document with photos, video, and the date/weather details — then get the breach tarped. Mitigation is required by your policy; permanent repairs wait for the adjuster.

ACV vs RCV — which do I have?

Check your declarations page. RCV pays today's replacement cost (depreciation released after work); ACV permanently subtracts depreciation, which stings on older roofs.

Can a roofer cover my deductible?

No — offering to waive or rebate a deductible is insurance fraud. Treat it as an instant disqualifier and verify the contractor at search.ccb.state.or.us.

Should I always file a claim?

No. If the repair is below or near your deductible, filing pays little or nothing and still marks your claims history. Get an honest estimate first, then decide.

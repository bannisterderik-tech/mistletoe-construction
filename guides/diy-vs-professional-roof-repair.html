DIY vs professional roof repair: where the line actually is

Yes, we're a roofing company telling you what to DIY — and we're going to tell you honestly, because there's real work you can do from the ground, and a short list of things that put people in the hospital.

By Mistletoe Construction Oregon CCB #255729 Updated July 2026 7 min read

Let's get the conflict of interest on the table: we get paid to work on roofs, so "call a pro" from us is worth exactly as much as the reasoning behind it. So here's the reasoning, in full — what you can genuinely handle yourself, what you shouldn't, and why the line sits where it does. Spoiler: the line is mostly about gravity, not skill.

What a homeowner can safely do

All of this is real, useful roof care, and all of it happens with your feet on the ground or, at most, on a stable ladder at a single-story eave:

  • Ground inspections, seasonally and after storms. Walk the house perimeter. Look for shingles in the yard, granules piling in downspout splash zones, sagging gutter runs, and daylight where flashing meets walls. Ten minutes, four times a year, catches most problems while they're cheap.
  • The binocular check. A pair of binoculars turns your driveway into an inspection platform. Scan for lifted or cracked shingles, moss mats on north slopes, rusted or lifted flashing, and cracked rubber boots around pipes. This is legitimately most of what an inspector looks for — done from zero feet of elevation.
  • Single-story gutter cleaning. On a one-story home with a good ladder on firm level ground — and someone home while you do it — clearing gutters is reasonable DIY. Two stories up changes the math completely; that's what we're for.
  • Trimming reachable limbs. Overhanging branches drop the debris that feeds moss and clogs valleys. What you can reach safely from the ground with a pole saw, trim.
  • Documenting damage. After wind or a fallen branch: photos from multiple angles, dated, from the ground. If it becomes an insurance claim, that documentation is gold — our storm claims guide walks through the whole process.
  • Attic checks. A flashlight in the attic after heavy rain — looking for damp sheathing, water stains, or daylight — is one of the best early-warning inspections there is, and it's completely safe.

What you shouldn't do — and why

  • Walk a wet, mossy, or steep roof. Ever. In western Oregon the roof is wet or moss-slicked from October to May and often beyond. Wet composition shingles are slick; mossy ones are slicker; and a steep pitch turns a slip into a fall with no recovery step.
  • Anything structural. Sagging ridgelines, soft spots underfoot, rotten decking, rafter issues — this is load-bearing carpentry with consequences, not a patch job.
  • The sealant smear. The classic DIY move: a leak appears, a caulk gun full of roofing tar goes up, black goop gets smeared over the suspect area. Three problems: it rarely seals the actual leak path, it often traps water in, and it can void your warranty while hiding the real problem from whoever has to fix it properly later. Unapproved gunk on shingles gives a manufacturer an easy claim denial — more on that in our warranty guide.
  • Pressure washing moss. Strips the granules, drives water under shingles, voids warranties. The safe method is gentle and works better anyway.
  • Shingle and flashing replacement. Not because it's intellectually hard — because it's done at height, and because small errors (wrong nail placement, broken seal strips on neighboring shingles) create leaks that surface a season later, somewhere else.

The fall math, honestly

We're not going to dramatize this, but we're not going to soft-pedal it either. Falls from ladders and roofs send a steady stream of homeowners to emergency rooms every year in this country, and falls are among the leading causes of accidental death at home. In construction — where people do this daily, with training — falls are the leading killer, which is why professional crews use harnesses, anchors, and rated equipment on jobs a homeowner would attempt in sneakers.

The economics are just as lopsided as the physics. A professional repair visit in Douglas County typically runs a few hundred dollars. A single ER visit starts well above that; a fall from eave height can cost a season of work or far worse. There is no roof repair on a residential house whose savings survive that comparison.

When DIY makes it worse

The most expensive repairs we do aren't storm damage — they're second repairs. Tar over a flashing leak that ran another two winters. A face-nailed shingle that channeled water into the deck. A pressure-washed slope that aged ten years in an afternoon. A failed DIY repair usually costs more than the professional repair would have, plus the professional repair.

Quick answers

What roof maintenance can I safely do myself?

Plenty, from the ground: walk-around inspections after storms, binocular checks of slopes and flashing, single-story gutter cleaning from a stable ladder with someone home, trimming reachable limbs, and photographing damage for your records. Ground-level vigilance catches most problems early.

Is it safe to walk on my roof?

In western Oregon, usually not. Roofs here are wet or moss-slicked most of the year, and falls from roofs and ladders are among the most common causes of serious home injuries. Pros use harnesses and anchors for a reason. Steep, wet, or two-story: never.

Can DIY repairs void my warranty?

Yes. Unapproved sealant smears, face-nailed shingles, and pressure washing are all grounds for claim denial — and tar often traps water while hiding the real leak from whoever repairs it properly later. Anything touching material or flashing should be done professionally and documented.

When should I call a professional?

For anything involving walking the roof, replacing shingles or flashing, active leaks, soft or sagging decking, or storm damage that may become a claim. A repair visit is typically a few hundred dollars — a fraction of one ER trip or one botched repair.

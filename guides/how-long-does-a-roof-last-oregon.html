Lifespan Guide

How long does a roof last in western Oregon?

The number on the shingle wrapper and the number our climate actually delivers are two different things. Here are honest lifespans by material, what shortens them, and how to know where your roof is on the clock.

By Mistletoe Construction Oregon CCB #255729 Updated July 2026 7 min read

"How much life is left in my roof?" is probably the question we get asked most, and the honest answer starts with an uncomfortable fact: western Oregon is hard on roofs. A shingle rated "30-year" in a lab doesn't know about 130 rainy days from October to May, followed by 86-degree bone-dry Julys that bake out whatever moisture the winter left behind.

That wet/dry cycle — swelling, drying, expanding, contracting, year after year — ages asphalt faster than most of the country. Add moss, and you can lose a decade. Here's what to actually expect.

Realistic lifespans by material, here

MaterialTypical life in western OregonNotes
3-tab asphalt shingles~12–18 yearsThe budget option, and it shows. Lighter, lower wind rating, single-layer construction. Rarely worth it here — the small savings up front disappear in lost years.
Architectural asphalt shingles~20–25 yearsThe regional workhorse. Thicker laminated construction, better wind rating, roughly ten more years of life than 3-tab. Algae-resistant (copper-granule) lines matter a lot in this climate.
Metal (standing seam or panel)~40–70 yearsSheds moss, needles, and rain beautifully; shrugs off the wet/dry cycle; Class A fire-rated assemblies available. Costs roughly 2–3x asphalt up front. Often the last roof a house needs.

If you're weighing that last row seriously, our metal vs asphalt guide runs the honest 40-year math, and the metal roofing page covers what installation involves.

What shortens a roof's life here

  • Moss, moss, moss. The Umpqua Valley's signature roof problem. Moss mats lift shingle edges, hold water against the surface, and dam drainage. An unmanaged moss load can turn a 25-year roof into a 15-year roof — and it's typically excluded from warranty coverage as a maintenance failure. Our moss guide covers removal and prevention in detail.
  • Poor attic ventilation. A roof cooks from below as much as above. Trapped heat in summer accelerates shingle aging; trapped moisture in winter condenses on the underside of the deck and rots it quietly. Bad ventilation can also void manufacturer warranties — more on that in our warranty guide.
  • Tree debris. Fir needles and oak leaves in valleys and gutters hold moisture against the roof for months. A clogged valley is a slow leak waiting for a hard rain.
  • Deferred small repairs. One lifted shingle or failed pipe boot is a $300 fix. Two winters later it's a sheet of rotten decking and a ceiling stain.
  • Layover installs. Shingles installed over old shingles trap heat and hide deck problems. Douglas County doesn't allow them anymore — full tear-off is required — but plenty of older roofs out there are layovers living short lives.

What extends it

  • Moss control on a schedule. An annual fall treatment before the October rains is the single most cost-effective habit for a shaded Oregon roof. Professional moss removal typically runs $250–$600 locally — cheap insurance on a five-figure roof.
  • Clean gutters and valleys. Water that drains doesn't damage. Simple as that.
  • Prompt small repairs. Fixing flashing and boots the season they fail keeps water out of the structure. Our roof repair crew handles most of these in a single visit.
  • Annual inspections. Most roof killers are visible a year or two before they leak. That's the whole premise of our Home Care Membership — annual roof and gutter inspection, seasonal storm checks, and a photo report every visit, from $49/month with no long-term contracts.

What to do at every age: a decision table

Asphalt roof ageWhat to do
0–10 yearsMaintain. Moss treatment, clean gutters, trim limbs. Any leak is likely a flashing or installation issue — repair it, and check whether workmanship warranty covers it.
10–15 yearsStart annual inspections. Repairs still make clear financial sense. Watch north-facing slopes for moss and valleys for granule buildup in the gutters.
15–20 yearsRepair selectively. A localized problem is still worth fixing; widespread curling, cracking, or bare spots mean start budgeting. Get a written condition report so you're planning, not reacting.
20–25 yearsPlan the replacement on your schedule, not the roof's. Choosing your contractor and material in the dry season beats emergency-tarping in November. See what replacement actually costs locally.
25+ yearsYou're on borrowed time in this climate. Repairs at this age are usually money thrown at a roof that's failing everywhere at once. Time for a full replacement.

The honest version

We replace roofs for a living, and we'll still tell you: most roofs under 15 years old don't need us for anything but maintenance and the occasional small repair. Anyone pushing a full replacement on a young roof with one leak should be asked some pointed questions — we wrote a list of twelve.

Quick answers

How long does an architectural asphalt roof last in western Oregon?

Typically about 20–25 years here — often less than the number on the wrapper. The wet/dry cycle stresses shingles hard, and unmanaged moss can cut five to ten years. Good ventilation and moss control get you to the high end of the range.

Does moss really shorten a roof's life?

Yes. In Douglas County it's one of the most common causes of early roof failure — moss lifts shingle edges, holds water against the surface, and dams drainage. A 25-year roof can fail in 15, and warranties usually exclude moss damage as a maintenance issue.

Do metal roofs really last 50 years or more?

A properly installed metal roof here typically lasts 40–70 years. It sheds moss and debris far better than shingles and handles our climate cycle without granule loss. The trade-off is upfront cost — usually two to three times asphalt.

How do I know if my roof needs replacing or just repairs?

Age plus condition. Under 15 years with a localized problem: almost always repair. Past 20 with widespread granule loss, curling, or repeated leaks in different places: repairs are just delaying the inevitable. A free written inspection settles it.

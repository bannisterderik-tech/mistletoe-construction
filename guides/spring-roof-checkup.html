Seasonal Guide

The spring roof checkup: your roof made it. Now verify.

Between October and May, a Douglas County roof takes 30-some inches of rain, a winter of windstorms, and a few freezes for good measure. Spring is when you audit the damage — while every fix is still a small one.

By Mistletoe Construction Oregon CCB #255729 Updated July 2026 6 min read

Here's a framing we wish more homeowners used: your roof just finished a six-month endurance test. Around 130 rainy days, wind events that peeled shingles off half the county, freeze-thaw nights that work on every rubber seal, and a moss growing season running full tilt the entire time. If nothing is dripping, congratulations — but "not dripping" and "undamaged" are different claims. Spring is when you check which one is true.

The stakes are simple. Everything winter broke is currently small, dry-season-fixable, and cheap. Left alone through summer, the same list greets the October rains as open wounds. Here's the audit, item by item.

1. Lifted and creased shingles: winter's signature move

Walk your house perimeter — binoculars help, roof-walking doesn't — and scan for shingles that look wrong: corners curling up, tabs sitting proud of their neighbors, and especially the subtle one, a horizontal crease line across a shingle that's lying flat again.

That crease is the sneaky one. When a storm gust lifts a shingle past its limit, the mat fractures along the bend and the sealant bond breaks. The shingle settles back down looking fine from the street — but it's now a loose flap held by habit, and the next real windstorm tears it free. Creases, lifted tabs, and any bare spots where shingles went missing entirely are exactly what a targeted roof repair visit handles in an afternoon in May. Also check the ground and gutters below suspect areas: piles of granules are a shingle's way of confessing.

2. Pipe boots and flashing: what the freezes did

The rubber collars around your plumbing vents (pipe boots) live a hard life — baked all summer, then flexed by every freeze-thaw cycle all winter. Cold makes aged rubber brittle, and winter is when hairline cracks become splits. A split boot is one of the most common leak sources we find, and one of the cheapest fixes in roofing. Scan every roof penetration: boots for visible cracking or gaps at the pipe, chimney and sidewall flashing for lifted edges or failed sealant lines, skylights for cracked perimeter seals. Any of these caught in spring is a minor line item.

3. Moss: it spent all winter growing

Winter is moss's whole season here. That faint green haze on the north slope in November is a established carpet by April — moss does nearly all its growing during the wet months, and it emerges from winter at maximum strength. Spring is the time to assess and plan, and here's the honest timing note: heavy removal works best in late summer, when drought weakens the moss and it releases its grip. What spring is for: catching light growth early (a light treatment now stops the spread), and getting established mats on the calendar for an August/September professional removal. The full strategy — including why pressure washing is never the answer — is in our moss prevention guide.

4. Gutters: six months of loading

By spring, your gutters hold the winter's full sediment record: fir needles, decomposed leaves that dodged the fall cleaning, shingle granules, and moss fragments — all compacted by months of rain into a dense sludge. Check for sag (standing water and debris weight pull gutters off-pitch), overflow stains on the fascia behind them, and downspouts that trickle instead of gush. A spring gutter cleaning matters more than people think in this climate: clean gutters are also how you'll spot the granule shedding that flags shingle problems early. If every spring and fall brings the same shovel-work, gutter guards are the structural fix.

5. The inside evidence: ceilings and attic

Finish the audit indoors. Walk every room and look up: new ceiling stains, even faint tea-colored rings, are winter leaks announcing themselves. Then ten minutes in the attic with a flashlight — dark streaks or water staining on the sheathing, damp or matted insulation, any daylight through the deck. A stain with no active drip means a slow leak that ran all winter; small now, structural later. (If you ever find the active-drip version mid-storm, our roof leak emergency guide is the first-hour playbook.)

Why spring is repair-booking season

Three practical reasons to book winter's repair list now instead of "sometime":

  • Diagnosis is fresh. Winter damage is easiest to trace in spring, before summer heat re-seals lifted shingles just enough to hide them.
  • Conditions are right. Repairs want dry shingles — sealants and adhesive strips bond properly on a drying spring roof, and crews can walk it safely.
  • The calendar is open. Come September, every homeowner in the Umpqua Valley calls at once trying to beat the rain. Spring repairs skip the fall crunch entirely.

There's also the summer angle: Roseburg summers are bone-dry with July highs around 86°F, and months of hard UV bake shingles, dry out sealant lines, and stress every rubber component. That wet-winter/hot-summer whiplash is why local asphalt roofs age faster than the brochure says. A roof that enters summer with its winter damage repaired weathers the baking season far better than one carrying open cracks into it. And if winter's damage traces to a specific storm — a named wind event, a fallen limb — check our storm damage insurance guide before assuming it's all out of pocket.

The honest close: verify, don't assume

Most springs, on most roofs, this audit finds little — a boot to swap, gutters to clear, moss to schedule. That's the good outcome, and it's worth confirming rather than assuming: the expensive roof failures we see almost never come from one bad storm, but from small winter damage that sat unverified through a summer and met the next wet season unrepaired.

Quick answers

What should the spring check cover?

Lifted or creased shingles, cracked pipe boots and flashing, winter moss growth, loaded gutters, and new stains on ceilings or attic sheathing. Five items, one afternoon.

Why do creased shingles matter if nothing leaks?

A crease means the mat fractured and the seal broke — the shingle is loose and weakened even lying flat. The next windstorm removes it. Spring repair beats November tarp.

Spring or fall for repairs?

Spring, for winter damage: fresh diagnosis, dry-enough conditions for sealants to bond, and open schedules before the fall booking crunch.

Does summer sun really damage roofs here?

Yes — bone-dry Roseburg summers bake shingles and dry out seals, and the wet-winter/hot-summer cycle ages asphalt fast. Entering summer repaired is the point of the spring checkup.

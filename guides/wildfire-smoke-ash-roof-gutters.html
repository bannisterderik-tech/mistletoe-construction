Smoke Season Guide

Wildfire ash on your roof and gutters: the cleanup nobody explains.

Every smoke season leaves a gray film on Douglas County roofs — and almost nobody talks about what it does up there, or how easy it is to make it worse. Here's the honest version, from a crew based in Riddle.

By Mistletoe Construction Oregon CCB #255729 Updated July 2026 7 min read

If you lived through the 2020 Archie Creek Fire summer — or any of the smoke seasons since — you know the routine: weeks of orange sky, ash on the cars, ash on the deck. What gets missed is the surface you can't see from the driveway. Your roof is the biggest ash-collector on the property, and your gutters are where it all ends up.

Most fire-season advice covers air filters and go-bags. Fair enough. But since nobody covers the roof part, we will.

What wildfire ash actually is (and why that matters)

Wildfire ash isn't fluffy campfire ash. It's a mix of fine mineral particles and combustion residue, and two properties make it a roof problem:

  • It's abrasive. The particles are hard and gritty. Scrubbed against an asphalt shingle, they act like fine sandpaper on the granule surface — the roof's UV armor.
  • It's alkaline when wet. Add water and ash forms a caustic, high-pH sludge. Sitting in gutters or against metal flashing, that sludge can dull finishes and accelerate corrosion; on skylights and painted metal it can etch if left through the wet season.

A light dusting on shingles, left alone, mostly just washes away with the first rains. The real damage comes from two directions: heavy ash concentrating in gutters and valleys, and well-meaning homeowners cleaning it wrong.

The gutter problem: ash plus first rain equals cement

Here's the sequence that plays out across the Umpqua Valley every fall. Ash accumulates in gutters through August and September, mixing with fir needles and dry leaf litter. Then the first October storms arrive and turn that dry mix into a dense, gray, concrete-like sludge that packs into downspouts and outlets. Now your gutters are clogged at the exact moment the wet season starts — overflowing onto fascia, foundations, and walkways, and carrying alkaline runoff across everything below.

The fix is timing: flush the gutters after smoke season ends but before the first serious rain. Dry or barely-damp ash rinses out of a gutter easily; November sludge has to be dug out by hand.

⚠ Don't sweep it. Don't blast it.

The two instinctive cleanups are the two worst. Aggressive dry-sweeping grinds abrasive ash into the shingle surface and strips granules. Pressure washing does everything wrong at once — strips granules, drives water and ash slurry under shingle edges, and can void your warranty. Gentle is the entire game.

Safe cleanup, step by step

  1. Wear an N95 and wait for calm air. Stirred-up ash is exactly what you spent smoke season avoiding. Mask on, and don't do this on a windy day.
  2. Light ash: soft tools only. For a thin film, a soft-bristle brush used lightly, or a leaf blower on low from a ladder at the eave, moves most of it without grinding. Work with the slope, never scrubbing against the granules.
  3. Rinse low and slow. A garden hose with a regular spray nozzle — no pressure washer, ever — working from ridge down so you're pushing ash toward the gutters, not under shingle edges.
  4. Flush gutters and downspouts. Scoop the bulk, then run water until downspouts flow clear. This is the step that matters most; it's the difference between ash leaving your roof system and ash stewing in it all winter.
  5. Rinse what's below. Ash runoff is alkaline — give plants, decks, and painted surfaces under the downspouts a quick clean-water rinse afterward.

If the roof is steep, the ash is heavy, or you'd rather not do ladder work in a mask, this is a quick professional visit. Our gutter cleaning and roof cleaning crews handle ash-season flushes every fall.

When smoke season meets dry-moss season

Here's a genuinely useful quirk of the Douglas County calendar: smoke season and the ideal moss-treatment window are the same months. Late summer is when drought-weakened moss should be brushed off and treated — and it's also when ash is sitting on the roof. The good news: one gentle cleaning pass handles both, and the same "brush softly, rinse low, never pressure wash" rules apply to each. The caution: don't let an ash layer talk you into extra scrubbing over mossy areas, where shingle edges are already stressed. Our moss prevention guide covers that side of the job in detail.

Attic vents: where smoke season sneaks indoors

Soffit, gable, and ridge vents are open airways by design, and during heavy smoke events fine ash drifts through them and settles on attic insulation. After a bad season it's worth a flashlight check; a heavy gray layer on the insulation is rare but worth knowing about.

The bigger reason to care about vents is embers. In an actual fire event, unscreened vents are one of the top ways homes ignite — embers ride the wind straight into the attic. Fine metal mesh (1/8″) over vent openings blocks embers and cuts ash intrusion, and it's one of the details insurers increasingly look for alongside a Class A roof. The post-fire-season inspection (and why October is the moment)

Quick answers

Does ash damage shingles?

Mostly through wrong cleanup — abrasive particles grind off granules when scrubbed, and wet ash forms an alkaline sludge that's hard on metal. Left alone, a light dusting mostly rinses away harmlessly.

How do I clean it safely?

Soft brush or low leaf blower for dry ash, a low-pressure hose rinse working top-down, then a full gutter and downspout flush. N95 on, pressure washer never.

Can ash get into the attic?

Yes, through soffit, gable, and ridge vents. Fine mesh screening (1/8″) cuts ash intrusion — and matters far more as ember protection in a real fire.

Do I need a post-fire-season inspection?

In this valley, yes — and it conveniently doubles as your pre-rain fall inspection. One October visit flushes the ash and readies the roof for the wet season.

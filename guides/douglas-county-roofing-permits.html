Roofing permits in Douglas County, Oregon: what homeowners need to know

When you need a permit, why the county won't let anyone shingle over old shingles, how inspections work, and why unpermitted roof work comes back to bite you at sale time.

By Mistletoe Construction Oregon CCB #255729 Updated July 2026 8 min read

Permits are nobody's favorite subject. But if you're planning a reroof in Roseburg, Riddle, Myrtle Creek, or anywhere else in Douglas County, the permit rules shape what your contractor can legally do, how the job gets inspected, and what shows up in your property records when you eventually sell. Ten minutes of reading now can save you a genuinely painful surprise later.

One honest note up front: building rules get updated, and the county — not a roofing company's website — is the final word. Everything below reflects what we deal with on real jobs as of mid-2026, but for anything where the details matter, confirm with the Douglas County Building Department at douglascountyor.gov. We'll flag the spots where that's especially true.

When a roofing permit is required

The working rule in Douglas County: a permit is generally required once roof work exceeds one roofing square — 100 square feet. In practice, that means:

  • Full replacements: permit required. Every reroof we do in the county gets one. There's no realistic way to replace a whole roof under the threshold.
  • Small repairs: usually exempt. Swapping a handful of wind-blown shingles or patching around a vent typically stays under one square and doesn't need a permit.
  • Bigger repairs: it depends. A repair that opens up a large section — say, replacing a whole valley or a storm-damaged slope — can cross the 100 sq ft line. Your contractor should make this call and document it.
  • Each structure counts separately. Reroofing the house and the detached shop generally means separate permits, one per structure.

Cities inside the county (Roseburg, Sutherlin, and others) may administer their own building programs, so the office you deal with can differ by address even when the rules rhyme. Again — the county's website or a quick phone call settles it, and a good contractor already knows which jurisdiction your address falls under.

The no-overlay rule: full tear-off required

Here's the rule that surprises the most people: Douglas County does not allow shingle-over-shingle overlays. You can't nail a new layer of asphalt shingles over the old one to save money. A reroof means a full tear-off down to the deck.

And honestly? Good. We'd tear off anyway, even where overlays are legal, because overlays are a false economy:

  • You can't inspect what you can't see. Tear-off is the only chance to find rotted sheathing, hidden leaks, and failing flashing — and fix them for a few hundred dollars instead of discovering them through your ceiling in five years.
  • Overlays telegraph every flaw. New shingles laid over curled old ones look lumpy from day one and seal poorly.
  • Double layers cook shingles. Two layers hold more heat, which ages asphalt faster — a real issue in our 86-degree bone-dry summers.
  • Fasteners need clean deck. Nails driven through an old layer seat inconsistently, which is exactly what you don't want in a wind event.
  • You pay for it twice. The next crew has to tear off two layers instead of one, and the disposal bill doubles.

⚠ If a bid says "layover" or "overlay"

Walk away. In Douglas County that bid describes work the county doesn't allow — which tells you the contractor either doesn't know the local rules or doesn't plan to pull a permit. Neither is someone you want on your roof. Here's how to verify any Oregon contractor's license in two minutes.

Local requirements worth knowing about

Two more Douglas County specifics come up on nearly every reroof:

  • Kick-out flashing at sidewalls. Where a roof edge dies into a vertical wall — think a garage roof meeting the two-story section — the county requires kick-out flashing to throw water into the gutter instead of letting it run down inside the siding. Missing kick-outs are one of the most common causes of hidden wall rot we find, so this is a rule we'd follow even if nobody checked.
  • No wood shakes in the Wildfire Hazard Overlay District. Parts of Douglas County — much of the forested edge, including areas hit by the 2020 Archie Creek Fire near Glide — sit inside a Wildfire Hazard Overlay District where wood shakes and wood shingles are prohibited. If you're in or near the wildland edge, plan on a Class A assembly: architectural asphalt or metal roofing. Whether a specific parcel is inside the overlay is a question for the county's maps, so confirm your address before you fall in love with a roofing material.

Requirements like edge metal specs and underlayment details also apply and occasionally get revised — one more reason the person pulling your permit should be a local contractor who tracks this stuff for a living.

Who pulls the permit? (Your contractor should.)

Oregon homeowners can pull their own permits for work on their own home. But when a contractor is doing the work, the contractor should pull the permit — it's part of the job, like ordering the shingles. At Mistletoe Construction, permit fees and paperwork are built into every roof replacement estimate; you never touch a form.

Be wary of a contractor who asks you to pull an "owner permit" for work they're performing. That maneuver shifts code responsibility onto you and often signals the contractor can't pull permits under their own license — because they don't have one in good standing. It's a two-minute check to find out: look them up on the CCB.

How the inspection process works

A permitted reroof isn't just paperwork — it comes with independent eyes on the work. The typical flow:

  1. Permit issued before work begins, listing the structure, roofing material, and scope.
  2. Mid-job inspection when required. Depending on the scope, an inspector may need to see the deck, underlayment, or flashing before it's covered up. Your contractor schedules this so the crew isn't standing around.
  3. Final inspection once the roof is complete. The inspector checks the visible details — edge metal, flashing, ventilation, fastening pattern where verifiable — and signs off.
  4. The record lives forever. That signed-off permit becomes part of your property's file with the county: dated, inspected proof of when the roof was replaced and that it met code. Keep your copy with your warranty documents.

Inspection steps and scheduling vary a bit by jurisdiction and job — one more "confirm with the county" item, and one more thing a local contractor just handles.

What unpermitted work costs you later

The county rarely drives around hunting for unpermitted reroofs. The reckoning almost always comes at sale time:

  • Oregon's seller disclosure form asks directly whether work was done without required permits. Answering falsely creates real legal exposure; answering honestly hands the buyer a negotiating hammer.
  • Buyers' inspectors and agents check. A "new roof" with no permit on file makes every buyer wonder what else was cut. Some lenders and insurers get twitchy about it too.
  • Fixing it after the fact costs more. Options range from after-the-fact permits (with fees, and possibly required corrections or partial uncovering of work) to price concessions at closing. None are cheaper than doing it right the first time.
  • Warranty and insurance friction. If a roof failure traces back to non-code work, expect harder conversations with both your insurer and the shingle manufacturer.

If you've bought a home and suspect a past roof was unpermitted, don't panic — call the Douglas County Building Department, explain the situation, and ask about your options. And if you'd like a professional read on the roof's actual condition first, our repair team does honest assessments, and Home Care members get a documented annual inspection with a photo report — handy evidence of good maintenance whenever you do sell.

Quick answers

Do I need a permit to replace my roof in Douglas County?

Generally yes — permits kick in once work exceeds one square (100 sq ft), which every full replacement does. Small patch repairs usually don't need one. Confirm current thresholds with the county at douglascountyor.gov.

Can I put new shingles over old ones?

No. Douglas County requires full tear-off — no shingle-over-shingle overlays. That's genuinely better for your roof anyway: it's the only way to inspect and fix the deck underneath.

Who pulls the permit?

Your contractor should, as part of the job — we do on every reroof. A contractor asking you to pull an owner permit for their work is a red flag worth investigating.

What if past work was unpermitted?

It typically surfaces at sale via Oregon's seller disclosure. Contact the county building department about after-the-fact options before you list.

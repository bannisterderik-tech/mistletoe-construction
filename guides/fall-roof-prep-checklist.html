Seasonal Guide

The fall roof prep checklist for Douglas County.

Around here, "winterizing" isn't about snow — it's about seven straight months of rain. Here's the September–October ritual that decides whether your roof coasts through the wet season or fights it.

By Mistletoe Construction Oregon CCB #255729 Updated July 2026 6 min read

Douglas County weather runs on a switch. From July through September it barely rains at all; then sometime in October the switch flips, and Roseburg, Riddle, and the whole Umpqua Valley settle in for 130-odd rainy days and 30–33 inches of water. Almost everything that goes wrong with a roof here goes wrong during the wet season — but almost everything that prevents it has to happen before.

That's why fall prep matters more in this valley than nearly anywhere else. You get a six-week window of dry, cooperative weather. Here's how to spend it.

1. Treat moss while it's weak (September)

Late summer is the one time of year moss is losing. After months of drought it's dry, brittle, and barely holding on — it brushes off shingles with a fraction of the damage that scraping a soggy winter mat causes. Just as important, a preventive moss treatment applied now sits on the roof waiting for the rain, protecting through the exact months when moss does nearly all its growing.

Miss this window and you're treating in spring, after the moss has had its whole growing season. Our full moss removal and prevention guide covers the how (and the pressure-washing mistake to never make); if your roof is shaded or already showing green, professional moss removal in September is the single best-timed maintenance dollar in this climate.

2. Time the gutter clean to the leaves (late October)

Here's the trick most people get backwards: don't clean gutters in early fall. Clean them after the first major leaf drop — usually late October here — so they're actually clear when the serious rain arrives. An early-September cleaning followed by the oak and maple dump just means doing the job twice, or worse, thinking it's done when it isn't.

Clogged gutters aren't cosmetic in this climate. When water overflows the gutter lip storm after storm, it soaks fascia boards, wicks under the first shingle course, and dumps concentrated runoff next to your foundation. A big share of the "roof leaks" we get called for in November are really gutter problems. If ladder work isn't your thing, our gutter cleaning service handles it — and if you're under big trees and tired of the ritual entirely, gutter guards turn a twice-a-year chore into an occasional rinse.

3. Trim the limbs hanging over your roof

Every branch over your roof does three bad things all winter: it drops the litter that clogs gutters and feeds moss, it casts the shade moss loves, and in a windstorm it's a battering ram parked over your shingles. Fall is the time to cut limbs back — the trees are heading into dormancy and the debris is done for the year.

The rule of thumb: nothing overhanging the roof plane, and ideally 6–10 feet of clearance. Small limbs are a homeowner job; anything big, near power lines, or requiring roof access is arborist territory. Either way, it's the biggest single environmental fix for a moss-prone roof.

4. Walk the perimeter: flashing, boots, and shingles

You don't need to get on the roof for this — binoculars from the yard work fine. You're looking for the small failures that become February leaks:

  • Pipe boots. The rubber collars around plumbing vents crack and split with UV age. A $20 boot fix now prevents a classic ceiling stain later.
  • Flashing. Look at chimneys, sidewalls, and skylights for metal that's lifted, rusted, or pulling away. Flashing failures cause more leaks than shingle failures.
  • Shingles. Scan for anything lifted, creased, cracked, or missing — especially along ridges and edges where summer heat and wind do their work.
  • Granules. Heavy granule piles at downspout outlets mean shingles are shedding their armor; worth a professional look.

Anything suspicious, photograph it from the ground and have it checked. A targeted roof repair in October is a small, scheduled job. The same repair in January is an emergency tarp situation — our winter roofing guide explains why wet-season repair options shrink fast.

5. Ten minutes in the attic

The attic tells you the truth about your roof. On a dry September day, take a flashlight up and check four things: water stains or dark streaks on the underside of the sheathing (evidence of past or slow leaks), wet or matted insulation, daylight showing through the deck, and soffit vents blocked by insulation. Then turn the flashlight off and let your eyes adjust — pinholes of light are punctures.

Ventilation matters more than people think here: a poorly vented attic traps winter moisture from the house below, and condensation can mimic a roof leak while quietly feeding mold. Blocked vents found in September are an easy fix.

Why "before the rain" is everything in the Umpqua Valley

Every item above shares one deadline. Moss treatment only works ahead of the growing season. Gutters only matter if they're clear when the water comes. Repairs need dry shingles for sealants and adhesives to bond. Even inspection is better in the dry — a roofer can walk a dry roof safely and thoroughly; a wet one, much less so.

Once the switch flips in October, you're not maintaining anymore — you're reacting. The whole point of this checklist is to never be reacting.

Quick answers

When should fall roof maintenance happen here?

September through mid-October: moss treatment and limb trimming first, gutters after the first big leaf drop, final checks before the October rains settle in.

Gutters — before or after leaf drop?

After the first major drop, usually late October, and again if heavy drop continues. Cleaning in early September means doing it twice; guards end the ritual entirely.

Why treat moss in late summer?

Drought-stressed moss brushes off easily, and a preventive treatment applied before October protects through the wet season — when moss does nearly all its growing.

What am I looking for in the attic?

Stains or streaks on the sheathing, wet insulation, daylight through the deck, and blocked soffit vents. Any of these in September is a cheap scheduled fix instead of a January emergency.

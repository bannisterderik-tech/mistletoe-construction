Roofing Glossary

Architectural Shingle

An architectural shingle is a laminated, multi-layer asphalt shingle — thicker, stronger in wind, and longer-lived than the old three-tab style.

An architectural shingle — also called a laminated or dimensional shingle — is an asphalt shingle made by bonding two or more layers of fiberglass-reinforced asphalt together. The extra material does more than look good: it makes the shingle heavier and stiffer, raises its wind rating, and gives the roof the shadowed, textured profile that mimics wood shake. It's the default product for quality reroofs across Douglas County, including the Owens Corning lines we install.

Why it matters on Oregon roofs

Two local realities favor architectural shingles. First, wind: winter storms rolling up the Umpqua Valley test every shingle's bond, and laminated shingles carry meaningfully stronger uplift ratings than three-tab. Second, lifespan: our wet-winter, scorching-summer cycle ages asphalt fast, and the thicker laminate typically buys about ten extra years — roughly 20–25 years of service here versus considerably less for three-tab. Most quality architectural lines also come standard with copper-bearing granules (algae-resistant shingles), a real advantage in the mossiest corner of the country, and in Class A fire-rated assemblies that matter under Oregon's wildfire rules.

When it fails, what it costs you

Even good architectural shingles die early if moss is left to lift their edges or if installation shortcuts skip the accessory system (starter strip, flashing, ventilation). A roof that should give you 25 years failing at 15 is a five-figure loss. Compare materials honestly in our metal vs. asphalt guide, and when it's time, our roof replacement page covers what a correct tear-off and install involves — Douglas County doesn't allow shingle-over-shingle overlays.

How long do architectural shingles last in Douglas County?

Plan on roughly 20–25 years with basic maintenance — less if moss goes unmanaged on shaded northern slopes, which is the most common early killer of asphalt roofs around Roseburg.

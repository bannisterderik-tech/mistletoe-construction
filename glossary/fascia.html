Roofing Glossary

Fascia

Fascia is the vertical trim board running along the lower edge of the roof, capping the rafter tails and giving your gutters something to hang from.

Fascia is the board you see when you look at a roof edge straight-on: it caps the exposed ends of the rafters, closes off the attic from weather and pests, and — most importantly for its own survival — it's what the gutters are screwed to. Above it sits the drip edge; behind and below it, the soffit finishes the underside of the overhang.

Why it matters in Douglas County

Fascia lives in the splash zone. Every fir needle and oak leaf that clogs a gutter in Roseburg or Sutherlin sends the next storm's runoff sheeting backward over the gutter's back edge — straight down the fascia board. Add roughly 130 wet days a year and a shaded roofline, and an unprotected fascia board can go from peeling paint to punky wood in a couple of seasons.

When it fails, what it costs you

Rotten fascia can't hold gutter screws, so the gutter sags, spills more water, and speeds up its own demise — a nasty little feedback loop. Left long enough, rot travels into rafter tails and soffit, turning a trim repair into a framing repair. The prevention is boring and cheap: keep the gutters clear (our gutter cleaning handles the fir-needle season) and repaint or wrap boards before bare wood shows. Replacing a run of fascia is routine work we often pair with siding and trim jobs.

Why is my fascia rotting behind the gutter?

Almost always: clogged gutters overflowing backward, missing drip edge, or both. The fix is the full path — clear or guard the gutters, add drip edge if absent, then replace the damaged board. Skipping the first two just rots the new board.

Related terms

Soffit · Eave · Drip edge · Downspout

Gutter pulling away from the house?

That usually means the board behind it is going soft. We'll check the fascia, gutters, and drip edge in one free visit.

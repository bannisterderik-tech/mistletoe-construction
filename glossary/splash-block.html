Roofing Glossary

Splash Block

A splash block is the sloped pad under a downspout that catches falling water and shunts it away from the foundation instead of letting it pool.

A splash block is a simple sloped pad — usually concrete or heavy plastic — set on the ground under a downspout outlet. It does three small jobs that add up: it takes the impact of the falling water so the stream doesn't excavate a crater in your soil, it stops mud from splashing onto the siding, and its channel shape carries the runoff a couple of feet away from the foundation before releasing it. It's the cheapest component in your entire roof-drainage system, and one of the most commonly missing.

Why it matters in Douglas County

Each downspout on an Umpqua Valley home concentrates thousands of gallons a year onto one square foot of ground. From October to May that spot never dries out. Without a splash block or extension, the water pools against the stem wall, saturates the soil, and finds its way into the crawlspace — the origin story of most of the musty-crawlspace and settling-porch problems in older Roseburg neighborhoods. Good roof work ends at the ground, not at the gutter: it's the last link in the chain that starts with the gutter apron at the roof edge.

When it's missing, what it costs you

The visible signs are a scoured hole under the outlet and mud staining on the skirting or siding. The invisible cost is bigger: chronically wet foundation soil invites settling, wood rot at posts and sills, and moisture that feeds mold under the house. Compared to that, a splash block is pocket change. While you're down there, make sure the gutters feeding it actually flow — clogged systems overflow everywhere except the splash block. Our gutter cleaning service handles the full path, and the winter roofing guide includes drainage in its pre-season checklist.

Which way should a splash block point?

Wide end away from the house, sitting on ground that slopes away, tucked snugly under the downspout elbow. A surprising number are found flipped, sunken, or pointing along the wall — worth a 30-second check each fall.

Roofing Glossary

Three-Tab Shingle

A three-tab shingle is a flat, single-layer asphalt shingle whose two cutouts form three uniform tabs — the classic budget roof covering.

A three-tab shingle is a single-layer asphalt shingle with two slots cut into its exposed edge, dividing it into three equal tabs. Laid up, the tabs create the flat, repeating brick pattern you see on millions of roofs built or reroofed before the 2000s. Compared with a laminated architectural shingle, a three-tab is thinner, lighter, cheaper — and shorter-lived, with lower wind ratings.

How it holds up in Douglas County

Honestly? Not great anymore. Our wet-winter/dry-summer cycle ages any asphalt roof quickly, and a three-tab's single layer has less material to give. Its lighter tabs are also the first thing flapping when a winter storm funnels wind up the valley, and each tab slot is a seam where wind-driven rain and moss get a head start — moss rhizoids love working under those exposed tab edges. Architectural shingles typically deliver about ten more years of life here plus better wind performance, which is why three-tab has mostly retreated to sheds, outbuildings, and the lowest-budget projects.

When it fails, what it costs you

Three-tab failure looks like missing tabs after storms, curled corners, and heavy granule loss in the gutters. Because many manufacturers have shrunk their three-tab lines, matching patches are increasingly hard to source — so a "small repair" often turns into a visible checkerboard. If your three-tab roof is 15-plus years old, it's worth pricing replacement honestly against repeated repairs; our Roseburg-area cost guide lays out what local jobs actually run. In the meantime, targeted repairs can buy time before the wet season.

Is a three-tab roof ever the right choice today?

For a heated home in Douglas County, rarely — the lifespan and wind-rating gap outweighs the modest savings. For a shop, shed, or short-horizon rental, it can still pencil out.

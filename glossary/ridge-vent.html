Roofing Glossary

Ridge Vent

A ridge vent is a low-profile vent running the length of the roof peak that lets warm, moist attic air escape while the soffit vents pull fresh air in.

A ridge vent hides in plain sight: a narrow slot is cut along the roof peak, covered by a baffled vent strip, and capped with ridge cap shingles so it reads as an ordinary ridge from the street. It's the exhaust half of a simple convection loop — warm air rises out at the peak, drawing cooler, drier air in through the soffit vents. Both halves have to work; a ridge vent with blocked soffits is a chimney with no air supply.

Why it matters in Douglas County

Attic ventilation earns its living twice a year here. All winter, household moisture drifts into the attic, and without exhaust it condenses on the cold roof deck — wet insulation, mold, and "leaks" on a roof that isn't leaking (our winter roofing guide covers the pattern). Then summer flips the script: under an 86-degree Roseburg sun, a sealed attic can hit well over 130°F, cooking asphalt shingles from below and shaving years off their life. Shingle manufacturers' warranties generally assume adequate ventilation, too.

When it's missing, what it costs you

The costs are quiet ones: mold remediation, soggy insulation that stops insulating, and a roof that ages out years early. Adding or upgrading ridge venting is easy and inexpensive during a roof replacement — the slot is cut while the roof is already open — so it's worth confirming it's in any reroof bid you compare.

Do ridge vents let rain in?

A properly installed ridge vent doesn't. Modern vents use external baffles and internal weather filters designed to shed wind-driven rain — tested well beyond what an Umpqua Valley storm throws at them. Leaks at a ridge usually trace to damaged caps or a botched installation, not the vent concept.

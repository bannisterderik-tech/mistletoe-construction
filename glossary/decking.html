Roofing Glossary

Roof Decking

Roof decking (also called sheathing) is the layer of plywood or OSB panels fastened over the rafters — the structural surface everything else on your roof is built on.

Roof decking is the wood you'd stand on if you stripped everything else away: 4x8 sheets of plywood or OSB (older homes may have solid board sheathing) nailed across the rafters. Every layer above — underlayment, flashing, shingles — depends on it, because it's what all the nails bite into. A roof is only as sound as the wood it's fastened to.

Why it matters in Douglas County

Our region's signature roof problem, moss, is really a decking problem in slow motion. A moss mat holds water against the shingles like a wet sponge through a six-month rainy season, and that moisture wicks through nail holes and seams into the wood below. Shaded north slopes in Roseburg and Myrtle Creek — the mossy ones — are exactly where we find soft decking. Keeping moss off (see our moss guide) is the cheapest decking protection there is.

When it fails, what it costs you

Rotten decking can't hold nails, sags between rafters, and shows up as wavy rooflines, spongy spots underfoot, or dark staining in the attic. The catch: nobody can fully inspect it until tear-off, which is why honest replacement estimates carry a per-sheet allowance for decking replacement rather than a too-good-to-be-true flat price. A few sheets is normal on an older roof; widespread rot adds real cost — our local cost guide explains how that math works.

How do I know if my roof decking is rotten?

Ground-level clues: a sagging or wavy roof plane, and shingles that ripple over low spots. From the attic: dark stains, mold, or daylight at the deck seams. Underfoot it feels spongy — but final confirmation only comes at tear-off.

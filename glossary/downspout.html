Roofing Glossary

Downspout

A downspout is the vertical pipe that takes the water your gutters collect and carries it down and away from the house's foundation.

A downspout (or leader) is the vertical pipe connecting the gutter outlet to the ground, where an elbow and extension — or a splash block — send water away from the foundation. Gutters only collect water; downspouts are what actually get rid of it. Sizing and spacing matter: a common rule of thumb is one downspout for roughly every 30–40 feet of gutter run, with bigger 3x4-inch pipes worth it on large or steep roof planes.

Why downspouts matter in Douglas County

Do the math on our climate: 30–33 inches of rain on an average-size roof is tens of thousands of gallons a year, nearly all of it funneled through a handful of pipes between October and May. And our downspouts fight a specific local enemy — Douglas-fir needles, which mat into felt-like plugs at gutter outlets and elbows. That's why gutter cleaning here means clearing the downspouts too, not just scooping the troughs, and why gutter guards that keep needles out of the system in the first place earn their keep.

When they fail, what it costs you

A clogged or undersized downspout backs the whole gutter up. Water then sheets over the edges and dumps along the foundation line — the leading cause of wet crawlspaces, settling walkways, and mud-splattered siding. In a freeze, an overflowing gutter becomes an ice load that pulls fasteners loose. And water standing in gutters wicks up under the roof edge at the eave unless a proper gutter apron is in place. Our winter roofing guide puts downspout checks high on the pre-season list; members of our Home Care Membership get seasonal debris checks handled for them.

Where should downspout water go?

At least several feet away from the foundation — via an extension, drainpipe, or splash block on grade that slopes away from the house. Straight down into the flowerbed against the wall is how crawlspace problems start.

Roofing Glossary

Standing Seam

Standing seam is a metal roof system of vertical panels joined by raised, interlocking seams — with every fastener hidden from the weather.

A standing seam roof is built from long vertical metal panels — usually steel or aluminum — whose edges turn up and lock together in raised seams an inch or more above the drainage plane. The clips and fasteners holding the roof down live inside those seams, concealed from sun and rain. That's the defining difference from cheaper "screw-down" metal panels: on standing seam, no screw head and no rubber washer is ever exposed to the weather.

Why it suits Douglas County roofs

Thirty-plus inches of rain wants to find every hole in a roof; standing seam simply doesn't offer any. The raised seams sit above flowing water, hidden clips let panels expand and contract through our hot, dry summers without loosening, and the slick surface sheds fir needles and discourages the moss that devours asphalt here. Metal assemblies are also available with a Class A fire rating — a genuine plus in wildfire country, as our metal vs. asphalt guide lays out. Expect a 40–70 year service life, roughly two to three roofs' worth of asphalt, at about 2–3x the upfront cost.

When it fails, what it costs you

Standing seam rarely fails on its own — problems almost always trace to installation: seams not fully locked, sloppy penetration flashing, or panels face-screwed where they shouldn't be. Because repairs mean working whole panels rather than individual shingles, fixing bad work is expensive, so installer skill matters more than brand. It also performs well on lower pitches where shingles can't go — see low-slope roof. If you're considering it, start with our metal roofing service page.

Is standing seam loud when it rains?

Not on a typical home. Installed over solid decking and underlayment with insulation below, a standing seam roof is barely louder than asphalt — the "loud tin roof" reputation comes from uninsulated barns and patio covers.
